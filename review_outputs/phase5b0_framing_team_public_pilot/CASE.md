## Exact initialization inputs

- `project_name`: `Score Disclosure Before Appeal`
- `requested_scope`: `Study a static application decision in which the underlying state is qualified or unqualified and neither side initially knows it perfectly. The institution observes an informative coarse score—low, middle, or high—and uses a fixed initial rule that accepts high and rejects low or middle. After rejection, the applicant privately observes an additional imperfect signal and chooses whether to pay a positive cost to appeal. An appeal generates a fresh review signal under a fixed technology and fixed final decision rule. Under decision_only a rejected applicant learns only the rejection; under score_disclosure the applicant also learns whether the rejected score was low or middle before choosing whether to appeal. The applicant values acceptance and pays the appeal cost; the institution cares about classification consequences and scarce appeal-processing resources. Frame one sharp economic-theory question about how this disclosure changes appeal selection and the institution's decision problem.`
- `framing_intent`: `Treat decision_only as the exact benchmark and disclosure of low versus middle as the smallest institutional departure, while holding the initial rule, appeal technology, and final review rule fixed. Choose the smallest nondegenerate information structure needed to ask whether the comparison is theoretically worthwhile. No aggregation of applicant and institution objectives, parameterization, mechanism, theorem, sign, welfare or policy ranking, equilibrium result, novelty claim, or target venue is fixed in advance.`

## Institution supplied by the researcher

- There is one static application decision. The applicant's underlying state
  is qualified or unqualified. Neither side is assumed to know that state
  perfectly at the outset.
- The institution observes an initial coarse score: low, middle, or high. The
  score is informative about the underlying state. Under the fixed initial
  rule, high is accepted and low or middle is rejected.
- After rejection, the applicant privately observes an additional imperfect
  signal relevant to the value of appealing and then chooses whether to pay a
  cost to appeal.
- An appeal produces a fresh review signal and applies a fixed final decision
  rule. The appeal technology and final rule are held constant across the two
  notification policies.
- Under `decision_only`, a rejected applicant learns only that the application
  was rejected. Under `score_disclosure`, the applicant also learns whether
  the initial score was low or middle before deciding whether to appeal.
- The applicant values acceptance and pays the appeal cost. The institution
  cares about classification consequences and scarce appeal-processing
  resources. No aggregate welfare criterion, policy ranking, parameterization,
  or equilibrium result is fixed in advance.
